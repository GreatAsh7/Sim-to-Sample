Project STS • Sim‑to‑Sample Robot Arm

Natural‑language lab automation for messy, real‑world benches.

Sim‑to‑Sample (STS) is a low‑cost 6‑DOF robot arm that turns plain English commands into safe, precise test‑tube handling—even on cluttered lab desks—using vision‑based policies instead of rigid, hard‑coded coordinates.

Designed for HMMV Labs

Water quality, environmental, agricultural, and food & beverage QC labs that need flexible automation between manual work and expensive gantry systems.

Problem

Labs are stuck between fatigue and rigidity.

High‑mix, medium‑volume labs still rely on manual test‑tube handling or rigid Cartesian gantries. Manual work introduces fatigue, data inconsistency, and safety risks. Traditional automation is expensive, blind to the environment, and only works if every tube is in a perfectly aligned grid.

There is a missing middle: a machine that is affordable, spatially flexible, and smart enough to understand both the bench and the operator’s intent.

Manual handling ⇒ fatigue & variability Gantry systems ⇒ rigid & blind Need: flexible, safe automation

Design Solution

A 6‑DOF, vision‑guided arm that listens to you.

STS is a custom 6‑degree‑of‑freedom robotic arm driven by serial‑bus servos or planetary motors with encoders for precise feedback. Instead of hard‑coded motion on a fixed grid, a vision‑based neural policy interprets the scene and executes natural language commands like “Pick up the red tube and place it in bin B.”

Unstructured Environment Ready

Handles cluttered, real‑world benches. The arm can approach from the side, maneuver around obstacles, and adapt to variable tube positions.

Natural Language to Motion

A vision‑language model maps diverse prompts (“pick red” vs “grab the red one”) to task IDs and safe motion plans.

Front‑End Control App

A web interface lets operators send commands, monitor status, and review test runs over WiFi via an Arduino‑based controller.

Built‑In Safety Envelope

Motion profiles, payload limits, and a hard kill switch are baked into the design to prioritize zero‑spill operation.

Core Capabilities

What the STS arm can do.

6 Degrees of Freedom

  • Base z‑axis rotation
  • First link rotation (shoulder)
  • Second link rotation (elbow)
  • Wrist rotation
  • Wrist pitch (up/down)
  • Claw open/close, sized for 0.75" tubes

Optional wrist extension (e.g., viper slide) can be added if the final design requires additional reach.

Why 6‑DOF beats gantries

  • 6‑DOF vs 3‑DOF: approach tubes from the side, not just straight down.
  • Obstacle‑aware: maneuver around racks and equipment, not just over them.
  • Mess‑tolerant: works on a “messy desk”; no rigid coordinate grid needed.

Designed use cases

  • Water & environmental testing labs
  • Agricultural & soil analysis labs
  • Food & beverage QC labs

Safety & Operating Envelope

Engineered around zero‑spill, low‑voltage operation.

Task & Payload

  • Optimized for 6" test tubes (0.75" diameter).
  • Payload limited to lightweight labware (< 500 g).
  • Single 6‑DOF arm; dual‑arm coordination out of scope.

Safety Constraints

  • Acceleration and jerk capped to prevent fluid turbulence.
  • Motion envelope limited (e.g., no unsafe angles past set limits).
  • Dedicated, easily accessible kill switch off the active arm body.

Power & Footprint

  • 12V DC low‑voltage system; no direct mains interaction.
  • Base fits inside an 8" × 8" square footprint.
  • Target throughput: ≥ 2 tubes per minute with accurate placement.

Testing & Validation

How we know STS works in the real world.

Generalization Testing

STS will select and place 15 tubes or similar objects randomly located within a 1.5 ft diameter work area under at least two lighting conditions.

  • Objects in 3 colors; at least 2× diameter spacing.
  • <= 30 seconds per placement; < 10 minutes total.
  • Full credit if ≤ 1 tube is mis‑binned.

Data & Training Quality

  • Data integrity checks for unoccluded, synced video + servo logs.
  • Policy loss monitored to ensure convergence.
  • Stress tests: 10+ minute continuous cycles for motor heat and wear.

Command Understanding

The vision‑language module is tested on varied prompts and edge cases:

  • Multiple similar objects ⇒ select by color and location.
  • Missing objects ⇒ detect absence and alert the user.
  • Obstructions ⇒ re‑view scene via additional angles or cameras.

Project Roadmap

From concept to cross‑tested prototype.

End of December

Foundations
  • Finalize bill of materials and place mechanical/electrical orders.
  • Lock in training platform and verify WiFi communication with Arduino.
  • Complete electrical schematics and torque calculations.
  • Finalize CAD for base, links, wrist, and claw.
  • Prototype front‑end app for arm control and monitoring.

End of February

First Integrated Arm
  • Build and test base + turret within 8" × 8" footprint.
  • Assemble shoulder and elbow linkage with gearing.
  • Finish wrist and test‑tube‑specific claw.
  • Integrate all subassemblies into first full prototype.
  • Complete wiring and begin hardware–software integration.

End of May

Validated System
  • Run full generalization tests on 15 tubes in varied lighting.
  • Stress‑test motors for continuous operation.
  • Verify safety constraints (speed, angles, zero‑spill behavior).
  • Refine mechanics for any hard‑to‑reach bin locations.
  • Confirm throughput of ≥ 2 tubes per minute with the front‑end app.
